Another day, another side project. My latest is Anthologies. It lets you package links to share stories. Or–more accurate–that’s what it will do when I launch it. I bought the domain two months ago and now I’m getting work started. I plan to share as much of my design and development process as possible. This post is a look at early design concepts and my thoughts on how I approach a new design.

First things first. If this idea sounds familiar, it’s because it’s been a thing before. In 2012, when I was on the Readability team, we designed and built Readlists. Readlists shut down in 2016. It happens. But I miss it and I know other people do to. I don’t think it’s a huge number of people, and that’s OK. My goal for Anthologies is to build a small tool that does one thing well. If only a few people use it, that’s A-OK with me. And much cheaper. (I plan to write about my approach to building this in a cost-efficient way as soon as I figure out how to do that.)

MVP

At its core, an Anthology is a title, description, and list of urls or “entries” as I’m referring to them. For the MVP, I’m also going to expand that to include details about each entry as they’re available. Each entry can have a title, excerpt, author, publication, and image. If each entry has those or not will depend on the contents of the page at the url. I know from experience that parsing pages for those details is unpredictable. So my designs need to be flexible.

Systems Check

Like a lot of designers, I sit down and get right to work on a new design by staring at fonts for an hour or two. For Anthologies, I wanted what I usually want from a typeface. I wanted sturdiness at small sizes and a good range of weights. I wanted a super heavy weight for stand-out titles. I also had a specific type hang-up this time around; I wanted a typeface with a two-story lowercase “g”. In early logo and UI sketches I kept drawing two-story g’s so I had it stuck in my brain and didn’t see a reason to fight it. I settled on Aaux Next. So far I’ve used the regular, medium, black, and ultra weights.

Another way I like to put off designing is to fiddle with grid configurations. Sketch makes this way too easy. After the proper amount fiddling I landed on a grid that seems to work well. The grid is seven–191 pixels wide–columns with ten pixel gutters. I set row gutters at twenty pixels, and sometimes every now and then actually place text on them the right way.

If you’re looking at the grid image you might notice it’s off-center to the right. That’s on purpose. You’ll see later that each mock-up includes a banner with branding on the left. I didn’t want that to use the grid. I only place the primary content on the grid.

With the correct amount of procrastination out of the way, I started designing. For content, I used The Longform Guide to Sad Retired Athletes. Their collections are a perfect match for the type of content I expect will make up most Anthologies.

1. Two-up

The first go at it. A lot went into this that you don’t see in the mock-up. Much shuffling of text and images happened to get to this point. I don’t know that this is “good”, but it felt like a complete thought. It felt resolved enough. It displays the Anthology content and provides links to the articles. That’s the goal.

The most valuable artifact of this iteration were the discovered interface patterns and components. These are building blocks for every later iteration. There’s a “cover”–made up of the title, description, and byline. The entries–made up of individual entry elements. And within those are smaller components. I think about these in Atomic design terms. The cover is a organism. It includes an “intro” molecule. The intro contains title, description, and byline atoms. And so on. These might not be the exact elements I end up with, but they’re a way for me to break down the interface into distinct parts.

Keeping my artboards, groups, and layers organized in Sketch helps me focus. Naming those items helps me get a jump on naming elements in HTML and CSS. The names I use in Sketch, I’ll use for future class names, files, etc.

The name of each design iteration here is the name I gave the artboard in Sketch. Those names might not stick and aren’t very descriptive, but they help me organize my thoughts.

The first iteration is always the hardest. With it out of the way I started having a lot of fun with the designs. The rest of the mock-ups are me asking; “what if…?” then designing answers. These aren’t meant to be separate themes to choose from. They’re exploration of possibilities. It’s me searching for interesting ways to display the same content. In that searching I find design solutions, but also more what ifs that lead to more design. This was also just good, fast design fun. I could fifty of these and keep going. But, I won’t because I want to ship this thing sooner than later.

2. Grid Dark

At this point I had a view elements I thought were working. Things I could iterate on at least. Both of the first concepts are dark and heavy though. For the next concept I wanted to trying lightening things up. Both in color and in design elements.

3. Text Light

Along with the lighter aesthetic, in this concept I changed my grid usage. In the first two concepts, I gave the cover three columns and the entries four. When I gave the cover image more prominence instead of being a background image, it felt like it needed more room to breathe. With that, I gave it another half a column and also reduced the entries to three-and-a-half columns.

Content-wise I introduced entry excerpts in this concept. From the beginning I had in my head that each entry works better with a excerpt, but those first two concepts worked better without, so I wanted to give it a try. I think they're helpful. The remainder of the concepts include excerpts.

In the future, I might make that a user-configurable item for per Anthology.

4. Text Light Imgs

This was the first concept I tried using an thumbnail image for each entry. When I saw them, I felt like the cover image could go away and let the title and description handle the work of being the cover. For this concept I also tried an off-white background color. I pulled this from Readability, Instapaper, Pocket, et al. In those tended to be something like a “sepia” theme.

I explored options for the Anthologies banner here as well. Here, I wanted to see if a stark contrast between banner and content would help the banner kind of detach and fade away. It feels counter-intuitive, but sometimes when an element is so much different than the rest of the design it can make it easier to ignore. Still not sure if it’s working or not, but it’s interesting.

5. Text Light Two

For this concept, I let myself wield more design elements. The scattered orange blocks, a few tech lines, the angle on the cover image, and the background behind the title. For those, I was thinking about something like a 1970s jazz album cover. Or at least how I remembered them looking. I also switched the title and entry headings to the Ultra weight of Aaux Next Ultra. They felt like they needed some extra heft.

With the introduction of more design elements on the cover it felt like the eye needed a break so removed the image thumbnails for each entry.

6. Spartan

After iteration five and all its elements, I wanted to try one the other way. For this concept I wanted to use the bare minimum. Text and images as content with no other design elements. One element stayed in place, the subtle border between the cover and entries. You might also notice all the font sizes increased. I wanted to see the title and each entry title take center stage. I was looking at–and taking cues–from the New York Times on this one.

At this point, this concept feels the strongest in terms of practicality and broad use.
